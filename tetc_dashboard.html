A Note on This Dashboard

This is a snapshot of the 9th grade year as it exists today, not as you remember it or planned it. Every analysis is built from the same lesson dataset — 23 lessons, each tagged with skills, concepts, design-process stages, and pillar alignment.

Where I've used judgment (especially in the SEP page and the pillar audit), the reasoning is visible in the data. Disagree with a tag? Tell me which one and I'll change it.

This dashboard is a thinking tool, not a report card. The most interesting findings are the surprises — the concept that was supposed to anchor the year but doesn't, the pillar that's missing, the lesson doing the most work. Those are the places worth leaning into.

What This Tells Us

The year is more balanced than fixated. No single concept dominates — the most-covered concept ("Cause and Effect") still only appears in about 22% of lessons. That's the signature of a year designed organically, with each lesson reaching for whichever concept fits the build, instead of returning to one drumbeat.

The shape of the data tells a story in three layers. There's a small core of recurring concepts (the top tier), a middle band of concepts that anchor a category but don't carry across the whole year, and a long tail of concepts that show up in only one lesson — sometimes by design, sometimes by accident.

!
Iteration is undercounted in the tags. It's flagged in 4 lessons but lives in many more — the cross-cutting skills sidebar in the worksheet captures this better. The data is honest about what was tagged, not what was experienced.
?
Data Analysis and Graphing dropped to 1–2 lessons after Proof: Acceleration was removed. The math/data backbone of the year is now thinner than it looks — worth knowing if you want students to feel competent with data later.
+
Long-tail concepts are revisit candidates. Phase Change, Fluid Dynamics, Sensors, and Circuits each anchor one lesson — perfect targets for a 10th grade lesson that calls back to a 9th grade experience.

What This Tells Us

The TETC 9th grade year is strong on engineering and applied physics, lighter on classical chemistry, and almost silent on classical computer science fundamentals. That's not a failure — it's a curriculum choice. But it's worth seeing on paper.

Some of the missing concepts genuinely belong in IPC or chemistry, not engineering — pH, stoichiometry, periodic table, atomic structure. Others (Newton's Three Laws, Ohm's Law, conservation of momentum) are physics-engineering bridges that show up implicitly in the lessons but never as named concepts students could recognize on a test.

!
Newton's Laws aren't named. Students experience Newton's 2nd law every time they tune a catapult and 3rd law every time the JudoBot flips back. Naming the law during the lesson would cost nothing and add transferable vocabulary.
~
The CS coverage is real but narrow. Programming logic shows up in Freeze Tag and Bucky Basketball, but loops, conditionals, and variables aren't explicit. Worth being aware of if students will face a CS course in 10th or 11th.
+
Many "missing" concepts could be added with one line of teacher framing — not a new lesson. "This is conservation of energy" during the rubber band car. "This is heat transfer" during the LN ice cream. The lesson stays. The vocabulary lands.

What This Tells Us

The TETC 9th grade year leans heavily on the build-and-test core of engineering practice — Planning & Carrying Out Investigations (SEP 3), Constructing Explanations / Designing Solutions (SEP 6), and Developing & Using Models (SEP 2) all show up in the majority of lessons. That's exactly what you'd expect from a hands-on program built around constructivism and failure-as-data.

The practices that show up least are also predictable: Argument from Evidence (SEP 7) and Obtaining/Communicating Information (SEP 8) almost never appear in current lesson tags — not because they're absent from the room, but because they're rarely the explicit focus of a lesson.

!
SEP 7 and SEP 8 are coverage gaps. A single capstone lesson where students defend a design choice in writing or pitch a redesign to peers would close both gaps at once — and would map cleanly to TEKS EDP I requirements for written/oral technical communication.
~
SEP 5 (Mathematics & Computational Thinking) thinned out after Proof: Acceleration was removed. Currently anchored by Velocity, Catapult, Hydraulics, and Bucky Basketball.
+
Robo Gripper hits the most practices in a single lesson (6 of 8). It's doing a lot of work — a strong candidate for the lesson architecture pattern you might want to clone elsewhere.
i
SEP 1 (Asking Questions / Defining Problems) is light by design. Most TETC lessons present the problem rather than asking students to define it. A scaffolding choice — but a path toward more student-driven lessons later.

What This Tells Us

This view is most useful for two questions: "if a kid loved this lesson, what should they try next?" and "which lessons could be sequenced together to compound learning?"

The strongest neighbors are usually within the same category — that's expected and confirms the categories make sense. The interesting cases are the cross-category bridges: a CAD lesson that pairs with a physics lesson, a chemistry lesson that overlaps with fabrication. These are places where students could be told explicitly: "this is the same idea you saw in X."

+
Bug Bots is a structural bridge. Its top neighbors include both Bucky Basketball (robotics) and Fidget (CAD) because all three involve iterating a physical design with motion. It could be the connective tissue linking three different parts of the year.
~
Lessons with very low neighbor scores are isolated. They might be intentionally novel, or they might be missing connection points to the rest of the year. Worth knowing which is which.
i
Sequencing intelligence: the strongest neighbors are good candidates for back-to-back ordering or even fused into a longer arc. The weakest neighbors are good for spacing apart in time so students get variety.

What This Tells Us

The framework you wrote and the year you actually built are aligned on two pillars and unbuilt on the third. Constructivism is everywhere. Design Thinking is in roughly half. Conversational AI is in zero lessons.

That's not a failure — it's an honest snapshot of where you are. The framework is aspirational by design, and the third pillar was added more recently than the first two. But seeing it on paper makes the gap concrete: "AI as thinking partner, not tool" is currently a stated value with no implementation, and that's the most actionable insight in this whole dashboard.

!
The Conversational AI pillar is unbuilt. Not under-built — unbuilt. The most concrete way forward is one prototype lesson where students use AI as a thinking partner, with explicit framing. The Stakeholder Interview Simulator you started building (Dana Reyes) is exactly this category — finishing it would close the largest gap in your framework's implementation.
+
The "tool disappears" rating is genuinely strong across most lessons. The exceptions (Intro to LightBurn, Do Nothing Machine, Dice) are appropriately tool-focused — a kid has to learn the tool before the tool can disappear. The rest of the year mostly clears the bar.
~
Failure-as-data is broadly present but weakest in the chemistry strand. Rainbow Lab, Boba, and LN Ice Cream are observation-driven, not failure-driven — by lab design. Worth noting if you want to add a chem lesson where students iterate based on what didn't work.
i
This is a framework integrity check, not a grade. The point is to see where the year matches the framework and where it diverges — both can be intentional. The diverging places are just decisions waiting to be made consciously.
